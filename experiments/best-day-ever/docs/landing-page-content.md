### Problem Section

**Section Headline**:

```
You know what you need to do. It's on your calendar. So why can't you start?
```

**Body Copy**:

```
For people with ADHD, the problem isn't knowing what to do — it's starting. Digital calendars are great for scheduling, but they're flat, forgettable, and buried behind notifications. They don't help you initiate.

Paper does. Writing by hand, using your favorite pen, feeling the page under your fingers — these sensory experiences create momentum that screens can't match.

But who has time to copy their calendar onto paper every morning? That takes the exact executive function ADHD makes difficult.
```

**Problem Points** (3 items, short and punchy):

- **Physical planners are blank** — Passion Planner and Erin Condren are beautiful, but you still have to copy your calendar by hand. That's the part you can't do.
- **Apps stay on screen** — Todoist, Notion, Google Calendar — great tools, but they don't give you something to hold, write on, and cross off.
- **"Just print your calendar"** — You've tried. The Google Calendar print view is a wall of tiny text nobody can work from.

### "Why Paper?" Section

**Section Headline**:

```
It's not old-fashioned. It's how your brain works.
```

**Body Copy**:

```
Research and lived experience both point to the same thing: for many people with ADHD, physical objects help where digital ones don't.
```

**Three points** (with small icons or visuals):

1. **Writing engages motor memory**
   ```
   The physical act of writing strengthens your commitment to a task in ways that reading a screen doesn't. When you write "call the dentist" by hand, you're more likely to do it.
   ```

2. **Paper doesn't compete for attention**
   ```
   A printed page on your desk doesn't send notifications, suggest YouTube videos, or hide behind 47 browser tabs. It just sits there, reminding you what's next.
   ```

3. **Sensory anchors help you start**
   ```
   The click of a pen. The color of a marker. The texture of the page. These small physical experiences help ADHD brains transition into work mode — they're initiation cues, not distractions.
   ```

## Design Notes

**Visual Style**:

- Warm, tactile, human — this is about paper, pens, and hands, not software
- Photography over illustrations where possible: real desks, real paper, real markers
- Avoid the typical "SaaS landing page" feel (no gradients, no floating UI mockups in space)
- Think: a well-lit desk photo with a printed plan, a good pen, and a cup of coffee

**Color Palette**:

- Warm neutrals (cream, soft white, warm gray) for backgrounds
- One accent color (something that feels like a favorite marker — a warm coral, a deep teal, or a golden yellow)
- Black text, high contrast for readability
- Print-inspired typography — clean serif or humanist sans-serif

**Typography**:

- Headlines: Bold, warm, approachable (not corporate)
- Body: Easy to scan, generous line height
- Consider a handwritten or marker-style font for small accents (section dividers, callouts) — but don't overdo it

**Imagery**:

- Template mockups should look like real printed pages with real handwriting on them
- Desk photography: paper, pens, markers, coffee, hands — show the ritual
- Avoid stock photos of people staring at laptops
- If showing the app interface at all, keep it small — the paper is the hero

**CTA Buttons**:

- High contrast against warm background
- Large, touch-friendly
- Rounded, feels approachable (not sharp/corporate)
- Accent color fill with white text

## Page Copy Variations (for A/B Testing)

### Variation A: Lead with ADHD

- Hero headline: "Your calendar, on paper. Built for ADHD brains."
- Leans into the ADHD framing from the start
- Risk: narrows the audience; reward: stronger resonance with primary segment

### Variation B: Lead with tactile

- Hero headline: "Your calendar, on paper. Ready in 60 seconds."
- Leads with the universal appeal (paper is better for doing) and reveals ADHD angle in the problem section
- Risk: less specific; reward: broader initial appeal, qualifies in body copy

### Variation C: Lead with pain

- Hero headline: "Stop re-copying your calendar every morning."
- Leads with the specific friction that the product eliminates
- Risk: assumes the reader already hand-copies their calendar; reward: instant recognition for those who do

**Recommendation**: Start with Variation B (current default). Test A and C as ad-matched variants if specific ad angles perform well.

## Post-Signup Email Sequence

1. **Immediate**: Welcome email — "You're on the list. Here's what we're building and why."
2. **Day 2**: "Quick question: what calendar app do you use?" (if they didn't answer in the form)
3. **Day 5**: "The science behind paper + ADHD" — short, genuine content piece that builds trust and reinforces the value prop
4. **Day 14**: "Progress update" — what's being built, when to expect access
5. **Launch day**: "Best Day Ever is ready. Here's your early access link."

**Tone**: Warm, direct, first-person. Not marketing-speak. Write like you're talking to someone who gets it because you get it too.
