# The Illuminator — Business Case

## Hypothesis

AI-assisted photo-to-embroidery—edge-aware subject treatment plus usable embroidery guides—can serve hobbyists who want custom work without the slow manual pattern shop flow, with revenue from digital files and optional print/kit add-ons.

---

## Why This Matters to Me

Embroidery is having a moment; the jump from a personal photo to a stitchable plan is still expert-heavy. Automating the painful middle could unlock a craft market that already pays for patterns and materials.

---

## Who It's For

Hobbyist embroiderers and crafters who want personalized designs from their own photos, are willing to pay for patterns or kits, and do not want a pro digitizing workflow (SewArt-style power user tools).

---

## What It Does

- ML/vision pipeline for subject emphasis and background treatment suited to embroidery
- Export paths: digital pattern, optional professional printing, optional physical kits
- Single-payment and add-on economics rather than a heavy subscription (for the solo-friendly wedge)

Not MVP: pro digitizing feature parity with desktop embroidery suites.

---

## Market

| Segment                                             | Size       | Basis                                            |
| --------------------------------------------------- | ---------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| Total market (AI photo-to-embroidery digital niche) | $3M–$15M   | Bottom-up segment sizing in market research      |
| Reachable segment                                   | $3M–$15M   | US-hobbyist digital tool focus (SAM ≈ TAM in MR) |
| Year 1 target                                       | $50K–$200K | Single purchases + add-ons                       |
| Year 3 target                                       | $500K–$2M  | Established SKUs, multiple channels              |

Global “embroidery industry” numbers are not the right ceiling—this competes in **digital pattern + hobby AI**, not industrial embroidery.

---

## Existing Options

| Option                         | Strength               | Limitation                                         |
| ------------------------------ | ---------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Etsy / custom pattern shops    | Trust, human craft     | Slow, variable quality, not scalable               |
| SewArt, Embird, etc.           | Pro tool depth         | Steep learning curve; not one-click from photo     |
| Canva / Adobe                  | Photo editing at scale | Not embroidery pattern output as a first-class job |
| Pre-made DMC / Dimensions kits | Quality materials      | No photo personalization                           |
| Big POD (Printful, etc.)       | Fulfillment            | Not embroidery-first conversion                    |

**Gap:** Consumer-grade **AI-assisted** “photo → embroidery plan” with optional physical fulfillment.

---

## Biggest Unknown

**Quality bar:** if outputs need heavy manual cleanup, you compete with human digitizers, not with software margin.

---

## Validation Plan

|                | Detail                                                                                                                |
| -------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Method         | Ship a tight MVP: a few test photos → pattern deliverables; measure edit time to “stitchable”                         |
| Traffic        | Craft communities, Etsy-adjacent audiences, content-led demos                                                         |
| Budget         | API/compute for vision; time for test stitching feedback                                                              |
| Success        | Users complete projects from generated patterns without pro intervention                                              |
| Decision point | If quality doesn’t hold for faces, small text, or busy backgrounds, narrow input constraints before scaling marketing |

**Status (hub):** Abandoned.

---

## Recommendation

**GO** for a **disciplined** prototype phase: pick 5 “hero” input types, measure time-to-acceptable pattern, and get real stitchers to validate. Without that, the business case is indistinguishable from a cool demo. Abandonment in the hub is fair until quality is proven on-thread.
